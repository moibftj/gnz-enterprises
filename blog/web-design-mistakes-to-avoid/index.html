The 17 Web Design Mistakes Most Sites Commit Before Breakfast

April 23, 202614 min readBy GNZ Enterprises

Mistakes, like pigeons, tend to arrive in committees. A page with one web design mistake is a stubbed toe. A page with seventeen is a full medieval plague. This essay is a polite post-mortem of the most common sins — the ones that quietly lose 40% of your traffic, 60% of your trust, and 100% of the reader's patience before the hero video has finished buffering.

Why "mistakes" is a kinder word than it sounds

A web design mistake is not a moral failing. It is, most often, an artifact of a team that has stared at the same page for six months and no longer sees it the way a first-time visitor does. It's giving familiarity blindness. It's giving we shipped the Figma, not the interaction. It is, in short, fixable — but only if named. So: let us name them.

The 17 cardinal web design mistakes (and the fix for each)

1. A hero that explains what you are named, not what you do

"Welcome to Acme" is not a hero. It is a doormat. The visitor, in the 1.3 seconds they have allotted you, needed a verb.

Fix: Replace the name-drop with a one-sentence value proposition that names the user, the outcome, and the mechanism. Example: "Close more demos without writing the follow-up emails."

2. Seventeen calls to action, one conviction

Pages with five CTAs convert worse than pages with one. This is not opinion; it's the paradox of choice in a tweed jacket. When everything is clickable, nothing is.

Fix: One primary action per page. One secondary in the nav. The rest can rest.

3. Carousels in the hero, also known as "nobody's favourite slide"

Nielsen Norman Group has been begging the industry to retire hero carousels since the Obama administration. The average click-through on slide 2 is ~1%. Slide 3, less. Slide 4, go to bed.

Fix: Pick the strongest slide, delete the rest, say thank you.

4. Autoplay video with sound

Respectfully: no. This is giving unsolicited opinion in an elevator. It cost you the visitor the moment their speaker flinched awake.

Fix: Autoplay fine, but muted with captions. Consent is a conversion lever.

5. Text over busy imagery with no overlay

Contrast is not a suggestion; it is a WCAG math problem. A photograph behind your H1 with no scrim is a font flex that your reader's retina is paying for.

Fix: 40–70% dark overlay, duotone, or a solid side panel for the copy. Contrast ratio ≥ 4.5:1 for body; 3:1 for large text.

6. Nav menus with twelve top-level items

Twelve top-level items is not navigation. It is a confession. It says: we cannot prioritise, so we hope you can.

Fix: Seven items, ideally. Five is chef's kiss. Group siblings under parents. Use mega-menus for real depth; don't fake it.

7. Every button looking like every other button

Primary, secondary, and ghost buttons should be visually distinct in fill, weight, and colour so the eye can assign hierarchy without effort.

Fix: One filled primary. One outlined secondary. One ghost tertiary. Done.

8. Infinite scroll on a site that should have pages

Infinite scroll is for social feeds and other time-traps. If your site has a footer — and it should — infinite scroll means the user will never see it.

Fix: Pagination, or "Load more" buttons. Keep the footer reachable.

9. Form fields asking for information you don't need

Every additional form field is a toll booth on the conversion highway. Ask for phone number "just in case" and watch your lead flow gain a headwind equal to a 17th-century schooner's.

Fix: Email. Maybe name. Maybe role. Everything else can be a follow-up email.

10. CTA buttons labelled "Submit"

"Submit" is what you do at customs. Users want to get the ebook, start their free trial, book the demo.

Fix: Verb + outcome. "Download the guide." "Start my trial." Never "submit."

11. Loading slower than three seconds on mobile

Every second past three costs you roughly 12% of mobile traffic. Past five, the visitor has mentally moved to a competitor who compressed their images. Core Web Vitals is a scorecard Google reads aloud to itself when ranking you.

Fix: AVIF/WebP images. Lazy-load below the fold. Kill hero videos heavier than 1.5 MB. Preload hero fonts. Audit in Lighthouse monthly.

12. "Contact us" as the entire conversion strategy

If the only way to engage is a form and a three-day wait, you're running a 1998 web design mistake in 2026 clothing.

Fix: A secondary path — "See pricing," "Watch 90-second demo," "See examples" — that doesn't require a human's calendar.

13. Hiding pricing as a "strategy"

Pricing hidden behind "request a quote" is fine for enterprise; a conversion disaster for everyone else. In the absence of numbers, users assume expensive or embarrassed.

Fix: At minimum, a starting price. At maximum, three tiers. If you genuinely can't, show a price range: "Typical projects: $8k–$40k."

14. Blog posts with no internal links

A blog that doesn't link to itself is a graveyard of individually indexed tombstones. Google reads internal linking to understand topic authority; users follow links to bind themselves to your site.

Fix: 3–6 contextual internal links per post. Anchor text with intent, not "click here."

15. Social proof that is either everywhere or nowhere

Either testimonials are buried in a graveyard section, or they festoon every viewport like Christmas lights in a haunted house. Both lose trust.

Fix: One hero-adjacent strip (logos or a quote). One deeper testimonial section before the final CTA. Two moments, maximum.

16. No 404 page worth its name

A generic "Page Not Found" in Times New Roman is a missed brand moment and a dead end.

Fix: Branded 404. Search bar. Three links to your best posts. A joke, if the brand allows.

17. A cookie banner that hostage-negotiates with the viewport

Cookie banners covering 40% of the screen with a giant "Accept all" and a coy "Manage preferences" tucked in underline-gray are dark-pattern behaviour — and, in 2026, a legal liability in a dozen jurisdictions.

Fix: "Accept" and "Reject" as equal-weight buttons. No dark patterns. Cover no more than a quarter of the viewport.

The five-minute mistake audit

Open your site and, in the voice of a suspicious aunt, ask:

  • Did I understand the offer in under three seconds?
  • Did I find a clear next step, or did I find seven?
  • Did anything make a noise I didn't ask for?
  • Is pricing discoverable in under two clicks?
  • Is the footer reachable without a panic attack?

If any answer is "no" — congratulations, you've found today's fix list.

Conclusion: a one-line sermon

Web design mistakes are not evidence of bad taste but of unexamined habit; inspect the page with the curiosity of a first-time visitor and half the list will fix itself before lunch.
