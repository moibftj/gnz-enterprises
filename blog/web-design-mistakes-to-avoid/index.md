# The 17 Web Design Mistakes Most Sites Commit Before Breakfast

Mistakes, like pigeons, tend to arrive in committees. A page with one **web design** mistake is a stubbed toe. A page with seventeen is a full medieval plague. This essay is a polite post-mortem of the most common sins — the ones that quietly lose 40% of your traffic, 60% of your trust, and 100% of the reader's patience before the hero video has finished buffering.

## Why "mistakes" is a kinder word than it sounds

A web design mistake is not a moral failing. It is, most often, an artifact of a team that has stared at the same page for six months and no longer sees it the way a first-time visitor does. It's giving *familiarity blindness*. It's giving *we shipped the Figma, not the interaction*. It is, in short, fixable — but only if named.

So: let us name them.

## The 17 cardinal web design mistakes (and the fix for each)

### 1. A hero that explains what you are *named*, not what you *do*

"Welcome to Acme" is not a hero. It is a doormat. The visitor, in the 1.3 seconds they have allotted you, needed a verb.

**Fix:** Replace the name-drop with a one-sentence value proposition that names the *user*, the *outcome*, and the *mechanism*. Example: "Close more demos without writing the follow-up emails." Job done.

### 2. Seventeen calls to action, one conviction

Pages with five CTAs convert worse than pages with one. This is not opinion; it's the paradox of choice in a tweed jacket. When everything is clickable, nothing is.

**Fix:** One primary action per page. One secondary in the nav. The rest can rest.

### 3. Carousels in the hero, also known as "nobody's favorite slide"

Nielsen Norman Group has been begging the industry to retire hero carousels since the Obama administration. The average click-through on slide 2 is ~1%. Slide 3, less. Slide 4, go to bed.

**Fix:** Pick the strongest slide, delete the rest, say thank you.

### 4. Autoplay video with sound

Respectfully: no. This is giving *unsolicited opinion in an elevator*. It cost you the visitor the moment their speaker flinched awake.

**Fix:** Autoplay fine, but *muted* with captions. Let the user choose to listen. Consent is a conversion lever.

### 5. Text over busy imagery with no overlay

Contrast is not a suggestion; it is a WCAG math problem. A photograph behind your H1 with no scrim is a font flex that your reader's retina is paying for.

**Fix:** 40–70% dark overlay, or a duotone, or a solid side panel for the copy. Pick one. Your contrast ratio must be at least 4.5:1 for normal body text, 3:1 for large.

### 6. Nav menus with twelve top-level items

Twelve top-level menu items is not navigation. It is a confession. It says: we cannot prioritize, so we hope you can.

**Fix:** Seven, ideally. Five is chef's kiss. Group siblings under parents. Use mega-menus for catalogs with real depth. For shallow sites, do not fake depth to look serious.

### 7. Every button looking like every other button

Primary, secondary, and ghost buttons should be visually distinct — in fill, weight, and colour — so the eye can assign hierarchy without effort. If all your buttons are the same rectangle in three different colours, that is not a design system; that is a swatch book.

**Fix:** One filled primary. One outlined secondary. One ghost tertiary. Done.

### 8. Infinite scroll on a site that should have pages

Infinite scroll is for social feeds and other time-traps. If your site has a **footer** (and it should), infinite scroll means the user will never see it. Goodbye, contact link. Goodbye, policy pages. Goodbye, authority.

**Fix:** Pagination. Or "Load more" buttons. Keep the footer reachable.

### 9. Form fields asking for information you don't need

Every additional form field is a toll booth on the conversion highway. Ask for phone number "just in case" and watch your lead flow gain a headwind equal to a 17th-century schooner's.

**Fix:** Ask for the minimum. Email. Maybe name. Maybe role. Everything else can be a follow-up email. *Everything.*

### 10. CTA buttons labelled "Submit"

"Submit" is what you do at customs. Users don't want to submit. Users want to *get the ebook*, *start their free trial*, *book the demo*.

**Fix:** Verb + outcome. "Download the guide." "Start my trial." "Book my call." Never "submit" — that word has the charisma of a damp napkin.

### 11. Loading slower than three seconds on mobile

Every second past three costs you roughly 12% of the traffic on mobile. Past five, the visitor has mentally moved to a competitor who compressed their images. Core Web Vitals is not a suggestion — it is a scorecard Google reads aloud to itself when ranking you.

**Fix:** Next-gen image formats (AVIF, WebP). Lazy-load below the fold. Kill hero videos heavier than 1.5 MB. Preload your hero font. Audit in Lighthouse monthly.

### 12. "Contact us" as the entire conversion strategy

If the only way to engage with your business is to fill out a form and wait three business days, you are running a 1998 web design mistake in 2026 clothing. There should be a self-serve path somewhere — pricing, calculator, demo video, free tier. Anything that respects the visitor's urgency.

**Fix:** A secondary path — "See pricing," "Watch 90-second demo," "See examples" — that doesn't require a human's calendar.

### 13. Hiding pricing as a "strategy"

Pricing hidden behind "request a quote" is fine for enterprise. It is a conversion disaster for everyone else. Your competitor's pricing is one tab away. The user's default assumption, in the absence of numbers, is that your product is either expensive or embarrassed.

**Fix:** At minimum, a starting price. At maximum, three tiers. If you genuinely cannot share pricing, show a price *range*. "Typical projects: $8k–$40k." That sentence is worth more than any contact form.

### 14. Blog posts with no internal links

A blog that doesn't link to itself is not a blog. It's a graveyard of individually indexed tombstones. Google reads your internal linking to understand topic authority; users follow links to bind themselves to your site.

**Fix:** 3–6 contextual internal links per post. Link from old posts to new ones when you publish. Use anchor text with intent, not "click here."

### 15. Social proof that is either everywhere or nowhere

Either your testimonials are buried in a graveyard section at the bottom of the page, or they festoon every viewport like Christmas lights in a haunted house. Both lose trust.

**Fix:** One hero-adjacent social-proof strip (logos or a quote). One deeper testimonial section before the final CTA. Two moments, maximum. Three if you're feeling generous.

### 16. No 404 page worth its name

A generic "Page Not Found" in Times New Roman is a missed brand moment and a dead end. The 404 is where a user stumbled — the least you can do is catch them.

**Fix:** Branded 404. Search bar. Three links to your best posts. A joke, if the brand allows. If you're GNZ Enterprises, a literary quip. If you're a bank, restraint.

### 17. A cookie banner that hostage-negotiates with the viewport

Cookie banners that cover 40% of the screen with a giant "Accept all" and a coy "Manage preferences" tucked in underline-gray are dark-pattern behavior. It is also, in 2026, a legal liability in a dozen jurisdictions.

**Fix:** "Accept" and "Reject" as equal-weight buttons. No dark patterns. Cover no more than one-quarter of the viewport. Remember: GDPR, CCPA, and the recent WCAG 3.0 draft are all watching.

## The five-minute mistake audit

Open your site, and in the voice of a suspicious aunt, ask:

- Did I understand the offer in under three seconds?
- Did I find a clear next step, or did I find seven?
- Did anything make a noise I didn't ask for?
- Is pricing discoverable in under two clicks?
- Is the footer reachable without a panic attack?

If any answer is "no" — congratulations, you've found today's fix list.

## Conclusion: a one-line sermon

Web design mistakes are not evidence of bad taste but of unexamined habit; inspect the page with the curiosity of a first-time visitor and half the list will fix itself before lunch.
