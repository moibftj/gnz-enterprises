When to Redesign Your Website: 10 Signs It's Stopped Loving You Back

April 20, 202614 min readBy GNZ Enterprises

Every website has a life cycle. Born full of promise. Launches on a Tuesday. Performs for two years. Develops CSS barnacles for three more. Then sits slumped in a digital bathrobe, muttering about how it used to be loved. If you're reading this, your site is probably in the bathrobe phase.

First — what counts as a "redesign"?

  • The patch — copy updates, one new page. $500–$3,000. One week.
  • The refresh — new typography, palette, hero, re-skinned components. $5,000–$25,000. 4–8 weeks.
  • The restructure — new IA, content, design system, same platform. $25,000–$100,000. 10–16 weeks.
  • The full redesign — new platform, IA, design system, brand application. $75,000–$500,000+. 4–10 months.

Sign #1: traffic is fine, conversion is not

The most commonly misdiagnosed problem. Before committing six figures, run a funnel audit: is the drop in a specific source? A specific page? A changed integration? A regressed load time? In ~60% of "we need a redesign" conversations, the real problem is one page or one step. That is a web design refresh task.

Sign #2: Core Web Vitals red for two quarters

Direct Google ranking factor since 2021. Direct conversion factor ~7% per second of LCP regression. Targets: LCP < 2.5s, INP < 200ms, CLS < 0.1. Try a dedicated perf engagement ($8K–$25K) first; if that can't hit thresholds on the current stack, re-platform.

Sign #3: not mobile-friendly in any serious sense

  • Hero text below 18px on mobile.
  • CTA buttons smaller than 44×44.
  • Hamburger nav with 11 unstructured items.
  • Horizontal scroll anywhere.
  • Forms that break when the keyboard opens.
  • Desktop-dimension images squeezed to fit.

Sign #4: your brand has moved on and your site hasn't

Sales sends case studies instead of the site. Marketing sends prospects to Notion. Recruiting suffers. Site is a deck generation behind the company. Usually a refresh or restructure, not a full redesign. 6–10 weeks, $30K–$90K.

Sign #5: you've outgrown your CMS

  • New pages require dev tickets.
  • Content team writes in Docs and hands off.
  • You need features the CMS cannot do.
  • Every release fights plugin compatibility.

A 10-day CMS audit saves or justifies six figures ~40% of the time.

Sign #6: your information architecture is a crime scene

  • 247 pages. 180 get under 3 visits/month.
  • Multiple pages cannibalize each other for keywords.
  • Users visit one page and leave.
  • Site search returns nothing useful.
  • Orphaned pages — no internal links pointing in.

A website audit ($3K–$10K) that classifies every page as keep/merge/rewrite/redirect/delete is the single most valuable deliverable.

Sign #7: analytics tell you nothing useful

GA4 partial; cookie consent tangled; nobody names last quarter's top converting page; attribution broken. Fix measurement first — $5K–$20K — before you spend $200K redesigning against broken data.

Sign #8: accessibility is a legal problem

WCAG 2.2 AA is the effective legal floor in 2026. ADA suits in the U.S. are a cottage industry; EAA enforcement in the EU is real. If basic screen reader nav fails, color contrast is under 4.5:1, or modals trap keyboards — get a pro audit. Remediation over $25K usually makes redesign the cheaper correct answer.

Sign #9: content is stale, thin, or AI-flavored generic

Google is aggressively penalizing shallow, unoriginal, unsupervised-AI content. A design refresh will not fix this. A content refresh will — $10K–$60K on your top 40 pages. Design without content is decoration without a dinner.

Sign #10: the site is emotionally embarrassing to its own team

Sales sends PDFs instead of links. Recruiters apologize for the site. Leadership winces in board meetings. The embarrassment is ambient; the underlying issues are specific. Find the specifics before concluding "we need a redesign."

The decision matrix

Choose the patch when

You have one broken page; a specific metric is down for a specific reason; content is out of date; the site is fundamentally healthy.

Choose the refresh when

IA and platform are sound; brand or visual system is the problem; you need to modernize look and feel.

Choose the restructure when

Content and IA are a mess but the platform survives; perf problems are solvable on the current stack; you need a new design system but not a new brand.

Choose the full redesign when

Platform can't support what you need; brand has shifted; analytics need a clean slate; 12-month ROI is positive.

The pre-redesign checklist

  1. Specific KPIs with baselines.
  2. Written business case tying KPIs to revenue.
  3. Content inventory — keep/merge/rewrite/redirect/delete.
  4. 12-month CWV and Lighthouse history.
  5. Analytics audit.
  6. Accessibility audit with remediation cost.
  7. CMS assessment — keep vs. re-platform.
  8. Ballpark budgets for refresh / restructure / full redesign.

Conclusion: a one-line sermon

A website redesign is not a makeover; it is a business decision, and the only correct reason to start one is that the smallest cheaper alternative has already been tried or honestly ruled out. Love your site by fixing it; redesign it only when fixing it has stopped being enough.
