Typography in Web Design: The Quiet Dictator of the Page

April 21, 202614 min readBy GNZ Enterprises

A page without considered typography is a speech delivered by a man in a borrowed suit — legible, perhaps, but nobody leans in. Typography in web design is not decoration; it is 95% of what the user experiences and roughly 100% of what they will remember. Respect it or be ignored. Respectfully.

Why typography is web design

Open any landing page and turn its images off. If what remains is intelligible, hierarchical, and — dare we say — a little seductive, the design works. If it reads like a spreadsheet with aspirations, you have been decorating, not designing. The fonts are doing the talking; the rest is wardrobe.

This is not new. Jan Tschichold said it in 1928. Wim Crouwel said it in 1964. Your landing page is re-saying it in 2026, whether you like it or not.

The five jobs typography actually does

  • Hierarchy. It tells the eye where to go first, second, and last — no arrows required.
  • Tone. Serif = heritage. Geometric sans = efficient. Humanist sans = friendly. Variable = flexing, respectfully.
  • Rhythm. Line height and measure make reading feel like breathing, not like trying to read in a moving car.
  • Trust. Good typography is a silent proxy for "these people know what they are doing."
  • Conversion. Body copy that people can actually read outconverts a hero of a thousand gradients. Every time.

The pairing rule your template violated

Pair one distinctive display face with one neutral text face. That's the rule. It has been the rule since Baskerville pressed ink onto rag paper and it will remain the rule long after the last designer has touched a MacBook.

Good modern pairings that do not embarrass themselves:

  • Fraunces + Inter — editorial warmth over clean workhorse.
  • Instrument Serif + Söhne — couture masthead, quiet text body.
  • Migra + Mona Sans — nearly-drag-queen headline, professional body.
  • Playfair Display + IBM Plex Sans — moody blogger, serious reporter.
  • General Sans variable for the whole site — one face, many weights. Also known as "the adult approach."

If your site pairs three display fonts, it is not brand confidence. It is a font menagerie, and your bounce rate is paying the zoo's upkeep.

The modular scale, explained without the jargon

Pick a ratio. Stick to it. Stop improvising font sizes at three in the morning.

  • H1 (hero): 56–80 desktop / 36–44 mobile / weight 700
  • H2: 36–44 desktop / 28–32 mobile / weight 600
  • H3: 24–28 desktop / 22–24 mobile / weight 600
  • Body: 17–19 desktop / 17–18 mobile / weight 400
  • Small: 14–15 desktop / 13–14 mobile / weight 400

A ratio of 1.25 (major third) is the dependable cousin. 1.333 (perfect fourth) for sites with editorial ambition. Anything above 1.5 is a concept album and reads like one.

Leading, measure, and the three numbers that save you

  • Body line height: 1.5–1.65. Lower is brutalist. Higher is a pediatrician's waiting room. Stay in the band.
  • Measure: 60–75 characters per line. More and the eye gets lost on return. Less and the rhythm becomes a stuttering text message.
  • Display line height: 1.05–1.15. Big type needs tighter leading or the letters start to drift like icebergs.

These three numbers are free, and most of the internet ignores them. Do not be most of the internet.

Hierarchy through contrast, not ornament

Size, weight, color, position. That is the quartet. Drop shadows, borders, and "section dividers" are ornamentation — they are what you reach for when the hierarchy isn't working. If your H2 needs a rule line above it to feel like an H2, your H2 is not yet an H2.

The three-weight rule

Use three weights, maximum: regular, medium, bold. A page using 300, 400, 500, 600, 700, and 800 weights isn't sophisticated. It's undecided.

Mobile typography: where sins are confessed

  • Body 17px minimum. 16px is a holdover from 2012. On 2026 screens it reads tired.
  • Do not tighten line height on mobile — loosen it. The thumb scrolls; the eye needs more room to re-find the next line.
  • No all-caps headlines longer than four words. On mobile they wrap like a tantrum.
  • Tap targets ≥ 44px. Text links in body copy should have generous padding on touch devices.

If your mobile typography forces pinch-zoom, that isn't a design choice. That is hazing.

Variable fonts are the adult choice

Variable fonts ship one file that covers every weight, width, and optical size. Result: a faster site, a tighter visual system, and the ability to interpolate between weights without dragging in a pile of woff2 files named like 2013 family photos.

If you are still shipping Inter-Regular.woff2, Inter-Medium.woff2, Inter-Bold.woff2, upgrade to Inter-Variable.woff2 and thank yourself in the morning. Core Web Vitals will write you a love letter.

The ten sins most commonly committed

  1. Inter 16px everywhere. Not a design. A capitulation.
  2. Lorem ipsum headlines never rewritten. A gravestone for your credibility.
  3. Justified body text on desktop. The rivers of whitespace are not aesthetic; they are a cry for help.
  4. Centered body paragraphs longer than two lines. The eye has nowhere to land.
  5. Six font weights on one page. Visually, a group chat.
  6. Decorative display font used for body copy. Against the Geneva Conventions.
  7. Tracking set to default on 72px headlines. Kern it (-2% to -3%) or apologize.
  8. color: #999 body text on #ffffff. WCAG is laughing. Users can't read it.
  9. Link underlines removed "for aesthetics." Replaced with a slight color shift nobody perceives.
  10. Two competing display faces in the hero. The UI equivalent of wearing two ties.

The 60-second typography audit

  • Open any page. Can you identify, at a glance, what is H1, H2, body, caption?
  • Select a paragraph. Count line length — is it between 60 and 75 characters?
  • Zoom to 200% — does the page still read, or does it sprint off the edge like a frightened cat?
  • How many distinct fonts? (Answer should be one or two.)
  • How many weights? (Three, maximum.)
  • Body size on mobile — 17px or above?
  • Contrast between body text and background — at least 4.5:1?

If you answered "no" to two of these, your typography is the reason your CTA is lonely.

Conclusion: a one-line sermon

Typography is not the decoration on a page — it is the page; the rest is weather, and a site that writes its letters with intention is a site that the reader, without quite knowing why, agrees to believe.
