The Web Design Process: 8 Steps From Napkin Sketch to Launch Day

April 21, 202614 min readBy GNZ Enterprises

Every agency on earth has a page titled "Our Process," and every one of them lies a little. Real web design is less linen and more spreadsheet — an eight-stage march with at least two breakdowns and one Slack message that begins "so about the scope."

Why the process matters more than the design

A mid-tier designer running a disciplined process will ship a better website than a world-class designer running none. Disciplined process catches scope creep, survives team changes, protects timelines, and turns feedback into a structured conversation. Treat the process as the product's first product.

The 8 stages

1 — Discovery & strategy

Before a pixel is moved: understand the business, the audience, and the one conversion event that matters. Deliverables: brief with the primary metric, 2–4 specific personas, competitor audit, quantified scope, rough sitemap.

2 — Information architecture & content strategy

A website is a content problem that looks like a design problem. Finalized sitemap, per-page purpose statements, content matrix, copy plan. This is where studios discover the client hasn't written copy in nine months.

3 — Wireframes (lo-fi, and mean it)

Grayscale, deliberately ugly. The right fights at this stage are structural: falsifiable hero claim, obvious scan path, purposeful CTAs, sane nav depth.

4 — Visual design & design system

Now the visual work begins. One or two brand-fit directions (not six — six is indecision), hi-fi designs for key templates, starter design system with tokens and components, responsive treatments for every breakpoint.

5 — Prototype & usability check

A static design file lies. A clickable prototype tested on 5 users for 20 minutes each surfaces 80% of structural problems at 5% of the cost of fixing them post-launch.

6 — Development

Handoff is where most designs go to die. A good handoff includes a complete design file with every state, exported tokens, documented components, a CMS source of truth, and all the empty/edge-case states.

7 — QA, accessibility, performance

  • Cross-browser: Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge, iOS Safari.
  • Responsive: at least three widths per breakpoint.
  • Accessibility: WCAG 2.2 AA.
  • Core Web Vitals on throttled 4G — LCP < 2.5s, CLS < 0.1, INP < 200ms.
  • Forms, payments, 404/500 behavior, copy proofing.

8 — Launch & post-launch loop

Launch is a week, not a moment. DNS cutover with a rollback plan, 301 redirects for every changed URL (this is 60% of why redesigns lose SEO), analytics migrated, Search Console resubmitted, post-launch monitoring for 14 days. Then the loop: 2-week, 4-week, and 6-week reviews.

Honest timelines

  • 5-page marketing site with a small design system: 5–8 weeks.
  • 10–20 page marketing site with a real CMS: 8–14 weeks.
  • Custom web app or ecommerce: 14–26 weeks.

The four process failures that sink projects

  • No locked scope.
  • Client copy arrives late (solve it by Stage 2).
  • Design by committee — name one decision-maker.
  • No staging environment — production is not a rehearsal space.

What healthy looks like

  • Weekly status emails exist and are boring.
  • Decisions are documented in a shared space.
  • The developer is in the Figma file by Stage 4, not Stage 6.
  • The client is relieved, not anxious, by the check-in.

How to hire with process in mind

  • "Walk me through your last project's timeline, including the delays."
  • "What does your discovery deliverable look like?"
  • "How do you handle scope changes mid-project?"
  • "Who makes the call on a designer-versus-developer disagreement?"
  • "What's the last project you refused to take on, and why?"

Conclusion: a one-line sermon

A beautiful website is the consequence of a disciplined web design process, not the other way around — and any studio that cannot describe their process in eight boring, specific stages is either hiding chaos or selling you vibes.
