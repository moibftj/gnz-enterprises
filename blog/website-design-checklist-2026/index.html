Website Design Checklist 2026: 27 Must-Have Elements Before You Launch

April 27, 202615 min readBy GNZ Enterprises

Most sites launch with something missing — not the obvious things, but the things that determine whether a visitor becomes a customer or a bounce statistic. This is the website design checklist we run against every site before the DNS switches over.

Why a checklist matters more than a mood board

The gap between a good-looking website and a working website is measurable in revenue. A site can be visually polished and still fail to generate a single lead because a form is broken, a page loads in six seconds, or the primary CTA is buried below the fold on mobile. This website design checklist is organized into five categories: Performance, Conversion, Trust, SEO, and Accessibility.

Category 1: Performance (items 1–6)

1. Core Web Vitals passing

Google's Core Web Vitals — LCP under 2.5 seconds, INP under 200ms, CLS under 0.1 — are both a ranking signal and a user experience signal. Run PageSpeed Insights on every key page before launch, not just the homepage.

2. Images properly compressed and formatted

Next-gen formats (WebP, AVIF) at appropriate compression. No image over 200KB on the homepage. Lazy loading on all images below the fold. This single item accounts for the majority of avoidable page-weight issues.

3. Mobile load time under 3 seconds

53% of mobile visitors abandon a site that takes over 3 seconds to load. Test on a throttled 4G connection, not your office fiber. The mobile experience is the primary experience for most audiences now.

4. HTTPS enabled and enforced

SSL certificate installed, http:// redirects to https://, no mixed-content warnings. A site without HTTPS is flagged as insecure by every major browser — a trust killer before the visitor reads a single word.

5. No broken links or 404 errors

Run a crawler (Screaming Frog or Sitebulb) across the full site and resolve every 404 before launch. A broken link on a services page during a prospect's first visit is a trust event you don't recover from.

6. Fonts loading from a performant source

Self-host web fonts where possible, or use font-display: swap to prevent invisible text during load. External font requests add a DNS lookup round-trip that can cost 200–400ms on cold connections.

Category 2: Conversion (items 7–13)

7. One clear primary CTA above the fold

The visitor should know what you want them to do within five seconds of landing. One CTA. Not three. Every additional option reduces the probability that the visitor takes any option at all.

8. Contact form tested and sending

Send a test submission from every form. Confirm notification emails arrive in the inbox — not spam. Confirm the success message or redirect works. This fails at launch more often than any other single item on this list.

9. Mobile tap targets sized correctly

Buttons and links need a minimum 44×44 pixel tap target. Navigation links too close together cause accidental taps. Test with a real thumb on a real phone — emulators lie about this.

10. Value proposition clear in the hero

The homepage hero must answer three questions instantly: what do you do, who is it for, and why should I care? If any answer requires scrolling, the hero is failing. Write the headline before you design the hero — then design around it.

11. Social proof visible early

A testimonial, case study, client logo strip, or star rating should appear within two scrolls of the homepage. Trust is built by showing others who already trust you — not by saying you're trustworthy.

12. Exit intent or lead capture (for high-traffic sites)

For sites generating meaningful traffic, an email capture mechanism — lead magnet, newsletter offer, or consultation booking prompt — converts passive visitors into a retargetable audience. Optional at launch; necessary as traffic grows.

13. Analytics tracking verified

Google Analytics 4 installed, configured, and sending data. Conversions set up for form submissions and key button clicks. If you can't measure it, you can't improve it — and the first month of real traffic is your most instructive data.

Category 3: Trust (items 14–18)

14. Real photography used (not generic stock)

Real photos of your team, workspace, or work outperform AI-generated and generic stock photography in every conversion test we have run. The web is full of the same smiling stock-photo businesspeople. A real photo signals authenticity that a polished composite cannot.

15. Privacy policy and cookie notice in place

Required by GDPR in Europe, CCPA in California, and increasingly by payment processors and ad platforms globally. A basic privacy policy is free to generate. The absence of one is a legal and trust liability.

16. Business information accurate and prominent

Phone number, email, physical address (if relevant), and business hours should be accessible from any page — in the header, footer, or both. A contact page that requires navigation loses calls.

17. "About" page with real people

The About page is consistently one of the top three most visited pages on any service-business website. It should contain real names, real faces, and a credible origin story. It is not a list of values — it is a human introduction.

18. Security badges or certifications visible (where applicable)

For e-commerce or any site taking payments: trust badges from your payment processor, SSL indicators, and relevant industry certifications reduce cart abandonment. Place them near the checkout CTA, not buried in the footer.

Category 4: SEO (items 19–23)

19. Unique title tags on every page

Every page needs a unique, keyword-forward title tag under 60 characters. The homepage title is not "Home" — it is your business name plus primary service plus city. Example: "Custom Web Design Services | GNZ Enterprises."

20. Meta descriptions written for every page

Under 160 characters. Written to earn the click, not just describe the page. Meta descriptions are your free ad copy in every search result — write them like you'd write a tweet that needed to generate a lead.

21. XML sitemap submitted to Search Console

The sitemap tells Google what pages exist. It takes five minutes to generate (most CMSs do it automatically) and significantly accelerates new page indexing. Submit it via Google Search Console on launch day.

22. Heading structure logical (H1 → H2 → H3)

One H1 per page, containing the primary keyword. H2s for major sections. H3s for subsections. This structure matters for screen readers, for featured snippet eligibility, and for the clarity of thought that tends to make content worth ranking in the first place.

23. Internal linking between related pages

Every services page should link to the contact page. Every blog post should link to at least one other blog post and one services page. Internal links distribute authority and give Google a map of how your content relates.

Category 5: Accessibility (items 24–27)

24. Color contrast meeting WCAG 2.1 AA

Normal text: 4.5:1 contrast ratio minimum. Large text: 3:1 minimum. Run every text-on-background combination through a contrast checker. Inaccessible contrast fails approximately 8% of male users with color vision deficiencies.

25. All images have alt text

Decorative images: alt="". Informational images: a description of what the image shows and why it matters in context. Alt text is the primary accessibility failure on most business websites and takes under an hour to fix comprehensively.

26. Keyboard navigation functional

Tab through every interactive element using only the keyboard. If you can't reach the contact form without a mouse, neither can approximately 7% of users with motor disabilities. Visible focus rings are a feature, not a bug.

27. prefers-reduced-motion respected

Any animations, parallax effects, or auto-playing video should be suppressed or slowed when the user's OS has reduced motion enabled. Vestibular disorders affect millions of users. The CSS required is two lines. There is no excuse for not including them.

Launching with confidence

A site that passes all 27 items on this website design checklist is not guaranteed to succeed — success depends on traffic, positioning, and the quality of your offer. But it is guaranteed not to fail for avoidable technical or design reasons. Most of the sites we see arrive for a redesign are losing leads because of items 7, 8, or 13. Fix those first. The rest is polish.

The website that passes the checklist is not the best-designed site in its category. It is the site that doesn't lose customers to solvable problems — and clearing that bar is more commercially valuable than winning a design award.
