B2B Web Design: Selling to the Humans Who Own Spreadsheets

April 23, 202614 min readBy GNZ Enterprises

B2B buyers are not, despite the enduring marketing folklore, a sombre species unmoved by delight. They are ordinary human beings with access to six-figure purchase orders and a calendar full of meetings they did not request. The job of B2B web design is to earn their ninety seconds, answer their three unspoken questions, and make the rest of the evaluation — which will involve a committee of five and two spreadsheets — less of a migraine for everyone.

Why B2B web design is not just "boring web design"

A B2C homepage competes against leisure. A B2B homepage competes against a Slack notification and a dental appointment. Attention is more expensive, buying cycles are longer, and decisions are made by committee — often in a meeting to which your homepage is not invited.

Three non-negotiable jobs:

  1. Prove, in under ten seconds, that the buyer is in the right place.
  2. Arm the internal champion with evidence to forward.
  3. Shorten the procurement chapter — which is where deals die — with pricing transparency, compliance signals, and docs.

Do those three and you have beaten 80% of your category, because 80% of your category is still running a 2019 "Schedule a demo" gauntlet.

The eight pages every B2B site needs

1. Homepage — the ten-second audition

  • H1: who you help, what outcome, how. "The revenue intelligence platform for RevOps teams managing >$50M ARR."
  • One social-proof strip (recognisable logos).
  • Three outcome-focused sections — outcomes with numbers, not feature bullets.
  • Secondary navigation for segments (by role, industry, company size).
  • Primary CTA: Book a demo. Secondary: See pricing or Watch 3-minute tour. Never both hidden behind the same form.

2. Product / features pages

One page per primary capability. Not one mega-page. Each page answers the "what does this do?" question from a specific buyer persona with screenshots, a diagram, and a two-minute loom.

3. Pricing — transparent or transparently explained

The #1 source of lost B2B conversions is a pricing page that says nothing. Publish tiers. If the price is "it depends," publish what it depends on and a range — "typical customers spend $2k–$12k per month." That paragraph closes the trust gap procurement officers are paid to widen.

4. Case studies / customers

B2B buyers buy with receipts. Every case study should include the customer's name, logo, industry, size; a quantified outcome; the named human who said the quote; three-to-five lines of context; and a downloadable PDF for the champion to forward to procurement.

5. Security and compliance page

If you sell to enterprise, this is not optional. SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, GDPR, DPA availability, subprocessor list, data residency options, pen-test cadence. The page's mere existence often qualifies you for vendor review. Its absence disqualifies you silently.

6. Documentation / API / help center

A product without public docs is giving we don't really ship. Link to docs from the main nav. Technical buyers — who are increasingly the decision-makers — browse your docs before they'll accept a demo call.

7. A blog with a clear content strategy

Not a corporate newsroom. Not a thought-leadership echo chamber. A buyer-intent blog that answers search queries your ICP types at 2 a.m. This is your top-of-funnel and your SEO moat.

8. About / leadership

Founder bio, leadership team, investor logos, company location, and a founding story that doesn't read like a TED proposal. Put faces on the site. Buyers are buying from humans.

The six pieces of evidence that actually move B2B procurement

  • Named logos (real, recognisable, permissioned).
  • Quantified outcomes ("reduced CAC by 23%," not "improved CAC").
  • Named humans in testimonials (first name + last name + title + company).
  • Third-party validation (G2, TrustRadius, Gartner, industry awards — where relevant).
  • Security posture (SOC 2, ISO, GDPR, pen-test, uptime SLA).
  • Interoperability (Salesforce, HubSpot, Snowflake, Okta). Buyers check this before price.

Every B2B page should have at least one of these six above the fold.

The ten B2B web design sins

  1. A hero that describes your platform but not the buyer's problem.
  2. "Schedule a demo" as the only CTA, across the entire site.
  3. No pricing, no range, no "starts at."
  4. Case studies with no numbers and no surnames.
  5. Jargon triple-stack ("hyperscale, mission-critical, turnkey") — the holy trinity of things nobody searches.
  6. A contact form with 11 required fields.
  7. No integrations page (or one that hasn't been updated since 2022).
  8. Bloggy content with no clear buyer's journey — a scattering of thought pieces, no pillar structure.
  9. Security page hidden in the footer in 9pt.
  10. Team page with stock-photo "team members" and no names. Trust dies twice: once for the visitor, once for search engines.

The B2B page-speed and SEO checklist

  • LCP under 2.5s on 4G mobile. Enterprise buyers open your site from a trade show with two bars.
  • Internal linking. Every page links to three relevant others. Pillar pages for each core topic.
  • Schema. Organization, Product, FAQPage, Article.
  • Canonical tags on industry / role variants.
  • Core Web Vitals. Enterprise IT teams run Lighthouse on vendor sites to form opinions. Pass the test.
  • No gated PDFs. Lose 200 leads a year, gain 2,000 backlinks and 40,000 organic visits.

The 60-minute B2B site audit

  • Pull up your homepage. Ask a non-technical friend what you sell. Can't answer in one sentence? H1 has failed.
  • Count your CTAs. More than two unique primary actions across the site — you're diluting.
  • Google "site:yourdomain.com". Fewer than forty pages? Your SEO footprint is a rounding error.
  • Open pricing on mobile. Under three seconds? Visible without seven scrolls?
  • DevTools → Network on the homepage. Under 1 MB excellent, 1–2 fine, 2–4 warning, 4+ emergency.
  • Security page — exists? Reachable in two clicks?
  • Integrations page — every integration listed with a working link?

Three "no"s is the project plan for next quarter.

Conclusion: a one-line sermon

B2B web design is not about looking corporate; it is about looking competent — a site that respects the buyer's afternoon, arms the champion with evidence, and makes the procurement officer look good for having found you.
