# B2B Web Design: Selling to the Humans Who Own Spreadsheets

B2B buyers are not, despite the enduring marketing folklore, a sombre species unmoved by delight. They are ordinary human beings with access to six-figure purchase orders and a calendar full of meetings they did not request. The job of **B2B web design** is to earn their ninety seconds, answer their three unspoken questions, and make the rest of the evaluation — which will involve a committee of five and two spreadsheets — less of a migraine for everyone.

This is a working manual on B2B websites that actually produce pipeline rather than decoration.

## Why B2B web design is not just "boring web design"

A B2C homepage competes against leisure. A B2B homepage competes against *a Slack notification and a dental appointment*. Attention is more expensive, buying cycles are longer, and decisions are made by committee — often in a meeting to which your homepage is not invited.

That means B2B web design has three non-negotiable jobs:

1. Prove, in under ten seconds, that the buyer is in the right place.
2. Arm the champion (the one person inside who actually cares) with evidence to forward internally.
3. Shorten the procurement chapter — which is where deals die — with pricing transparency, compliance signals, and docs.

Do those three jobs and you have beaten 80% of your category already, because 80% of your category is still running a 2019 "Schedule a demo" gauntlet.

## The eight pages every B2B site needs

### 1. Homepage — the ten-second audition

- **H1** stating *who* you help, *what* outcome, *how* you do it. Example: "The revenue intelligence platform for RevOps teams managing >$50M ARR."
- One social-proof strip (logos of recognisable customers).
- Three outcome-focused sections (not features — *outcomes with numbers*).
- Secondary navigation for segments (by role, by industry, by company size).
- Primary CTA: *Book a demo*. Secondary CTA: *See pricing* or *Watch 3-minute tour*. Never both hidden behind the same form.

### 2. Product / features pages

One page *per primary capability*. Not one mega-page. Each page answers the "what does this do?" question from a specific buyer persona, with screenshots, a diagram, and a two-minute loom.

### 3. Pricing page — transparent or transparently explained

The #1 source of lost B2B conversions is a pricing page that says nothing. At minimum, publish tiers. If the actual price is "it depends," publish what it depends on (seat count, volume, modules) and a *range* — "typical customers spend $2k–$12k per month." That paragraph alone closes the trust gap procurement officers are paid to widen.

### 4. Case study / customers page

B2B buyers buy with receipts. Every case study should include:

- The company's name, logo, industry, size.
- A *quantified* outcome ("reduced onboarding time 62%").
- The named human who said the quote.
- Three-to-five lines of context — *what* they tried first and why it didn't work.
- A PDF version, downloadable, that the champion can email to procurement.

### 5. Security and compliance page

If you sell to enterprise, this is not optional. SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, GDPR, DPA availability, subprocessor list, data residency options, penetration-test cadence. The mere *existence* of this page often qualifies you for vendor review. Its absence disqualifies you silently.

### 6. Documentation / API / help center

A product without public docs is giving *we don't really ship*. Even if it's on a sub-domain, *link to it from the main nav*. Technical buyers — who are increasingly the decision-makers — browse your docs before they'll accept a demo call.

### 7. A blog with a clear content strategy

Not a corporate newsroom. Not a thought-leadership echo chamber. A *buyer-intent* blog that answers the search queries your ICP types at 2am ("how to forecast revenue pipeline with low data," "how to structure RevOps team of 12"). This is your top-of-funnel; it is also your SEO moat.

### 8. About / leadership

B2B sales is still human sales. Founder bio, leadership team, investor logos if any, company location, and a founding-story paragraph that doesn't read like a TED proposal. Put faces on the site. Buyers are buying from humans.

## The six pieces of evidence that actually move B2B procurement

- **Named logos** (real, recognisable, permissioned).
- **Quantified outcomes** ("reduced CAC by 23%," not "improved CAC").
- **Named humans** in testimonials (first name + last name + title + company).
- **Third-party validation** (G2 / TrustRadius / Gartner / industry awards — only where relevant).
- **Security posture** (SOC 2, ISO, GDPR, penetration test, uptime SLA).
- **Interoperability** (logos of tools you integrate with — Salesforce, HubSpot, Snowflake, Okta). Buyers check this before they check price.

Every B2B page — *every* page — should have at least one of these six above the fold.

## The ten B2B web design sins

1. A hero that describes your *platform* but not the *buyer's problem*. ("AI-native revenue operations intelligence platform" — mate, who did this help?)
2. "Schedule a demo" as the only CTA, across the entire site.
3. No pricing, no range, no "starts at."
4. Case studies with no numbers and no surnames.
5. Jargon triple-stack ("hyperscale, mission-critical, turnkey") — the holy trinity of things nobody searches.
6. A contact form with 11 required fields.
7. No integrations page (or one that hasn't been updated since 2022).
8. Bloggy content with no clear buyer's journey — a scattering of thought pieces, no pillar structure.
9. Security page hidden in the footer in 9pt.
10. Team page with stock-photo "team members" and no names. This is how trust dies, twice: once for the visitor, once for the search engines.

## The B2B page speed and SEO checklist

- **LCP under 2.5s on 4G mobile.** Enterprise buyers open your site from a trade show with two bars. Be kind.
- **Internal linking.** Every page links to three relevant others. Pillar pages for each core topic.
- **Schema.** `Organization`, `Product`, `FAQPage`, `Article`. Your rich results depend on it.
- **Canonical tags.** Especially on industry / role variants ("for sales teams" / "for ops teams" of the same page).
- **Core Web Vitals.** Enterprise IT teams run Lighthouse on vendor sites *to form opinions*. This is a real thing. Pass the test.
- **No gated PDFs.** Yes, you'll lose 200 leads a year. You'll *gain* 2,000 backlinks and 40,000 organic visits. Do the math.

## The 60-minute B2B site audit

- Pull up your homepage. Ask a non-technical friend what you sell. If they can't answer in one sentence, the H1 has failed.
- Count your CTAs. If there are more than two unique primary actions across the whole site, you're diluting.
- Search your site in Google ("site:yourdomain.com"). How many pages? If it's fewer than forty, your SEO footprint is a rounding error.
- Open your pricing page on mobile. Does it load in under three seconds? Is the pricing visible without scrolling seven viewports?
- Open DevTools → Network. Your homepage ships how many KB? Under 1 MB is excellent, 1–2 MB is fine, 2–4 MB is a warning, 4 MB+ is an emergency.
- Security page — does it exist? Can a procurement officer find it in two clicks?
- Integrations page — does it list every integration with a logo, or are there dead links?

A "no" on three of these is the project plan for next quarter.

## Conclusion: a one-line sermon

**B2B web design** is not about looking corporate; it is about looking *competent* — a site that respects the buyer's afternoon, arms the champion with evidence, and makes the procurement officer look good for having found you.
