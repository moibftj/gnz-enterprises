A portfolio site is a paradox: it is a designer's most public self-portrait and, with statistically horrifying frequency, their most unloved project. The cobbler's children are clicking through a 2019 Squarespace template. Let us correct the record.

## What a portfolio is actually for

A portfolio is not a gallery. A gallery is for admiration; a portfolio is for hiring. Its job — the single job — is to move a viewer from "who's this" to "let's talk" in under 120 seconds. Write down the one action you want the viewer to take. Every decision descends from that sentence.

## The six archetypes that work in 2026

1. **The Serif Editorial** — display serif, generous whitespace, long-read case studies. Senior designers selling taste.
2. **The Brutal Index** — monospace, grid, no hero, projects with hover states doing the emotional labor. Product designers, developers-who-design.
3. **The Story-Told-in-Scroll** — single long page, scrolljacking done tastefully. Motion designers, interaction designers.
4. **The Portfolio-as-Product** — filter by service, sort by client, dark mode, actual case-study reader. Full-stack, PM-adjacent, senior roles.
5. **The Zine** — maximalist, opinionated layout, deliberately not a template. Illustration-heavy, brand designers, the confident.
6. **The Résumé-With-Images** — one-pager, three projects, a line of copy each, contact email. Senior designers getting inbound — the flex is the restraint.

## The ten rules across all archetypes

Three to six projects (not twelve). Case studies, not screenshots. Outcomes in numbers. Show the work you want more of. Your face and your voice somewhere. Contact in three places (nav, footer, end of each case study). Loads under 2.5s on 3G Slow. Accessible or you're lying about being a designer. Updated in the last six months. One distinctive gesture — not three.

## The case-study skeleton that lands interviews

The problem (two business-term sentences) → the constraint → your role (no "we" without scope) → the approach (show the process) → the decisions (three, with one rejected alternative) → the shipped outcome → the measurable result → what you learned or would do differently. Target 600–1,200 words per case study.

## Eight mistakes that close you in twelve seconds

Unskippable intro animation. No About page. A 2019 project still top of the grid. Design language inconsistent with the projects you're showing. Self-hosted fonts that FOUC badly. Password-protected projects with no preview. "Project #1, Project #2" naming. No opinions.

## Portfolio examples to study

Awwwards Site of the Day for the restraint buried in the maximalism, Lapa Ninja and onepagelove for conversion-minded curation, Read.cv for the résumé-as-profile archetype, Are.na channels tagged "portfolio" for the small-internet aesthetic, senior designers' personal domains for the ones that out-design the agency gallery.

## The 5-question portfolio audit

- Within 10 seconds, does the viewer know who you are and what you do?
- Within 30 seconds, can they find a project matching what they'd hire for?
- Within 60 seconds, can they email you without visiting a third page?
- Is the work you most want to do more of featured most prominently?
- Does the portfolio itself make the same design decisions the case studies claim you make?

## Verdict

A portfolio is the one design where the client is also the audience — and the designer who treats it with the same discipline and discernment as any other project is the one whose inbox fills up.
