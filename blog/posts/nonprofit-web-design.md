A nonprofit website is the only retail storefront most donors will ever visit — and most of them are designed as if the mission statement were the product. It isn't. The product is trust, converted into a gift. Let us design the storefront accordingly.

## The quiet math of a nonprofit homepage

Industry conversion rate on a nonprofit homepage sits between 1.1% and 1.8% — roughly one-third of a typical ecommerce site. Four numbers compound: recurring-gift share, average gift size, form-completion rate, page weight. Move any one by 10% and the whole fundraising model shifts.

## The five jobs a nonprofit site must do

1. **Tell a story a human can repeat** — if a donor can't describe the mission in one sentence, they won't send the link to their friends.
2. **Prove the money goes somewhere** — 990s, audited financials, Charity Navigator rating, outcomes with dates.
3. **Make giving take fewer than three screens** — anything more is a leak.
4. **Give volunteers a door** — not a PDF of instructions buried in a 2014 "Get Involved" page.
5. **Reassure institutional funders** — foundation officers read your About page looking for governance, not inspiration.

## Donation-page non-negotiables

- Three preset amounts + custom field (the presets anchor; the custom field converts).
- Monthly pre-selected or at least visually weighted — recurring is the math that saves the mission.
- Apple Pay, Google Pay, PayPal next to card (mobile donors quit forms, not wallets).
- No required account. A nonprofit asking donors to "create a password" is losing 20% right there.
- One line of impact micro-copy next to each amount ("$35 funds a week of tutoring for one student").
- 501(c)(3) reassurance visible: EIN, tax-deductibility, receipt-by-email promise.
- LCP under 2 seconds. A donor in the mood is a donor on a timer.

## Trust architecture

Board of directors with photos and names (not just initials). Most recent 990 linked in the footer. Charity Navigator, GuideStar, BBB Wise Giving badges where applicable. Dated impact numbers — "2,381 students served in 2025" beats "thousands served." A physical mailing address; grantmakers check. Staff faces on the About page — parasocial trust is the fundraising fuel of the age.

## Storytelling without exploitation

Agency (beneficiaries as narrators, not subjects), dignity (no "poverty porn" imagery), context (a story explains a system, not a stereotype), consent (release forms for every photograph, every name, every quote — not the audit, the ethic).

## Accessibility as ethics, not feature

Contrast 4.5:1 minimum. Keyboard navigation. Captions on every video. Screen-reader test on the donation form. Alt text on every outcome photo. Forms that work with autofill. 8th-grade reading level for donation pages (not the research report). If your site cannot be used by someone with low vision, you have quietly decided which donors matter.

## Content architecture that funders expect

A seven-page spine: Home, About/Mission, Programs (one page per major program), Impact/Outcomes, Financials/Accountability, Get Involved (donate + volunteer + events), News/Stories. Most nonprofit sites have forty-three pages; most donors read four.

## Six mistakes that cost you 30% of gifts

Autoplay video with narration. Giving page requiring a login. PDF newsletters in place of a news page. Mission statement longer than one paragraph. Stock photos of cheering diverse teenagers. A footer that doesn't repeat the donation CTA.

## The 10-step pre-launch checklist

- Donation form passes WCAG 2.2 AA and Apple/Google Pay both render.
- Homepage loads in under 2.5 seconds on 3G Slow.
- 990 and board page exist and are linked from the footer.
- Impact numbers dated within the last 12 months.
- Staff and board photos present, correctly credited.
- Every photograph has written consent on file.
- Recurring giving is preselected or visually weighted.
- Every program page has a "Donate to this program" CTA.
- Newsletter sign-up requires one field, not seven.
- Receipt email lands within 60 seconds of the gift, signed by a human.

## Verdict

A nonprofit website is not a brochure for the mission — it is the mission performing itself in HTML, and the ones that convert are those that treat the donor, the volunteer, the funder, and the beneficiary with the same unhurried respect.
